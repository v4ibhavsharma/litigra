## Factual Background

In **Narinder Kaur v. State of Punjab and Others**, CWP-7475-2020 (O&M), the Punjab and Haryana High Court examined the scope of family pension entitlements under the Punjab Civil Services Rules and reaffirmed the principle that statutory pension rights, despite their humanitarian importance, must be claimed strictly in accordance with prescribed conditions. Justice Harpreet Singh Brar's judgment provides important guidance on the limits of executive discretion and the necessity of statutory compliance in pension administration.

The petitioner's husband was appointed as a Clerk in the Market Committee, Patiala on 30.04.1991. However, his official career came to an abrupt and tragic end when an FIR was registered against him on 22.11.1992, just over a year after his appointment. This led to his suspension on 12.01.1993. Subsequently, he went missing and was eventually declared deceased in an encounter. The petitioner was thereafter appointed on compassionate grounds as a Clerk in the Market Committee, Ghanaur.

## The Claim Under Rule 6.17

Facing financial hardship, the petitioner filed a claim for family pension under Rule 6.17 of the Punjab Civil Services Rules. This rule prescribes the conditions under which the family of a deceased employee becomes entitled to receive pension benefits. The claim was rejected by the administering authorities on a straightforward ground: her deceased husband had completed only 43 days of actual service before his suspension and subsequent absence, far short of the statutory minimum required.

Rule 6.17 of the Punjab Civil Services Rules explicitly provides that family pension entitlements arise only where the deceased employee has completed at least one year of continuous service. Furthermore, the rule contains a critical qualification: periods of suspension are not counted towards the completion of service unless and until they are subsequently regularized by the competent authority. In the petitioner's case, no such regularization had occurred.

## The Supernumerary Post Argument

The petitioner sought to overcome this constraint through several arguments. Most significantly, she pointed to a supernumerary post that had been created for her deceased husband in 2013, more than two decades after his death. The administration had taken this step as a sympathetic gesture, intending to release salary for the actual 43 days of work he had performed. The petitioner contended that this belated creation of the supernumerary post amounted to a regularization of his service that would now entitle his family to pension benefits.

Justice Brar rejected this argument. The Court held that the retrospective creation of the supernumerary post was a limited gesture motivated by humanitarian concerns and was intended solely to ensure that the petitioner's deceased husband received payment for the work actually done. However, it could not be construed as a regularization of his entire service that would trigger pension entitlements. The distinction is legally significant and practically important: releasing salary for services rendered is administratively different from retrospectively regularizing a suspension and converting it into valid service for pension purposes.

## The Court's Analysis

The Court grounded its decision in established principles of pension law. The judgment relied on **UP Roadways Retired Officials Association v. State of U.P.**, 2024 INSC 555, and **Prabhu Narain v. State of U.P.**, 2004 (13) SCC 662, both of which establish that pension is not a bounty or ex gratia payment. Rather, pension is a statutory right that must be earned by meeting the prescribed conditions, and courts cannot extend pension entitlements beyond what the rules permit merely on grounds of hardship or sympathetic consideration.

This principle reflects a fundamental tension in administrative law: the need to balance rigid adherence to rules with equitable considerations for deserving cases. The Court acknowledged the petitioner's tragic circumstances but held that this acknowledgment, appropriate in other contexts such as compassionate appointment, could not override the explicit statutory requirements for pension entitlements. To do so would be to elevate humanitarian sentiment above the rule of law and would open the door to inconsistent administration of pension benefits.

The judgment also recognized that the petitioner had been afforded relief through compassionate appointment, which had provided her with income and service credit that would eventually lead to her own pension entitlements. While this might not fully address the loss of her deceased husband's pension, it represented the administration's attempt to balance compassion with statutory limitations.

## Key Principles

Justice Brar's decision underscores several important principles in service law. First, the distinction between administrative gestures and formal regularization matters significantly in pension law. Second, statutory conditions for pension entitlements are not mere guidelines but mandatory prescriptions that cannot be waived even in deserving cases. Third, courts have limited authority to extend pension rights beyond what the rules explicitly permit. These principles ensure that pension administration proceeds uniformly and fairly, preventing arbitrary extensions based on individual circumstances.
