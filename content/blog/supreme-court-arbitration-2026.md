The Supreme Court's recent decision on arbitration has clarified several procedural aspects of arbitration law in India. The ruling addresses the scope of court intervention in arbitral proceedings under Section 34 of the Arbitration and Conciliation Act.

The Court has narrowed the grounds on which courts may set aside an arbitral award. Mere errors of law or fact do not justify intervention. Instead, awards can be challenged only where they breach the rules of natural justice, are vitiated by fraud, or contain decisions on matters outside the arbitrator's jurisdiction.

The judgment also establishes a six-month timeline for courts to dispose of challenges to arbitral awards. This addresses the long-standing concern that enforcement delays undermine the efficiency advantages of arbitration.

The ruling clarifies that arbitrator immunity from civil liability is qualified. Immunity applies when decisions are rendered within jurisdiction and in good faith. However, gross negligence or manifest disregard of procedure falls outside this protection.

For commercial practice, these clarifications strengthen the utility of arbitration as a dispute resolution mechanism. Parties can expect faster enforcement and reduced exposure to challenge. This is particularly significant for contracts involving infrastructure, technology, and international trade.

The Court's approach balances finality of awards with fairness to parties. It limits satellite litigation while preserving recourse where fundamental procedural protections have been breached.
