The Punjab and Haryana High Court in **Ankit Agrawal v. State of Punjab** (CRM-M-14012-2024) considered the grant of bail in a Narcotic Drugs and Psychotropic Substances Act case where the quantity of drug recovered marginally exceeded the prescribed commercial threshold. The decision illustrates the court's nuanced approach to bail assessment in drug cases where the quantity differential is minimal and personal circumstances of the accused warrant consideration.

## Background and the NDPS Offence

**Mr. Ankit Agrawal** was arrested following the recovery of Alprazolam, a psychotropic substance regulated under the NDPS Act. The recovered quantity, while exceeding the quantity threshold that defines "commercial quantity," did so by only a small margin. The distinction between quantities constituting "personal consumption," "small quantity," and "commercial quantity" has significant implications for bail eligibility and the severity of punishment under the NDPS Act.

Under the NDPS Act, bail becomes more restrictive and difficult to obtain in cases involving commercial quantities. The statutory framework presumes that recovery of commercial quantities indicates involvement in trafficking or distribution rather than personal use. However, this presumption is not immutable and may be rebutted by evidence of personal circumstances and the proximity of the recovered quantity to the threshold.

## The Critical Distinction: Threshold Analysis

The High Court's decision pivoted on the recognition that while the recovered quantity technically fell within the commercial quantity bracket, the margin by which it exceeded the prescribed threshold was negligible. The court acknowledged the significance of this narrow differential in the context of bail assessment. Where quantities marginally exceed statutory thresholds, courts have exercised discretion to consider additional factors beyond the mechanical application of the drug quantity classification.

The judgment reflects judicial awareness that rigid application of quantity-based presumptions can sometimes work unfair results where the differential is minimal. The court accordingly looked beyond the bare quantitative assessment to examine the qualitative circumstances surrounding the arrest and recovery.

## Clean Antecedents and Personal Circumstances

The court placed substantial reliance on the fact that **Mr. Agrawal** possessed clean antecedents. The absence of prior criminal history constitutes a significant factor in bail assessment, particularly in drug cases where the quantity lies at the boundary of legal classification. Clean personal record, stable residence, established roots in the community, and absence of likelihood of absconding are traditional considerations that courts weigh against the presumptive seriousness suggested by the drug quantity.

The High Court found that the combination of clean antecedents, the marginally excessive quantity, and the overall circumstances of the case permitted the grant of bail notwithstanding the commercial quantity classification. The court imposed conditions ensuring the petitioner's continued availability for investigation and trial proceedings while granting him release.

## Bail Principles in NDPS Cases

This decision reinforces that bail in NDPS cases, while admittedly difficult to obtain, is not mechanically foreclosed by the quantity of drug recovered. The court retains discretion to assess each case on its individual merits. The quantity of narcotic substance is a significant factor but not necessarily the sole determinant. Personal antecedents, circumstances of arrest, evidence against the accused, likelihood of absconding, and other relevant considerations may influence the exercise of judicial discretion.
