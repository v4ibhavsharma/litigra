India's corporate governance framework has changed in 2026 with amendments to the Companies Act and associated rules. Companies should understand these changes to ensure compliance.

Board composition requirements have been expanded. Listed companies with assets above specified thresholds must now have at least 40% of independent directors with prior experience in technology, sustainability, or international business. This reflects the complexity of modern corporate operations and the importance of relevant expertise on boards.

Related party transaction controls have been tightened. Companies must now conduct valuations for all transactions with related parties exceeding specified amounts. Independent auditor approval is required in addition to existing shareholder approvals. The purpose is to prevent value transfers to connected parties while allowing legitimate transactions.

Disclosure obligations have expanded. Public companies must now disclose board diversity metrics, succession planning processes, and risk management frameworks in their board's report and annual integrated report. These requirements enhance transparency for investors and stakeholders.

The nomination process for independent directors has been clarified. Independence criteria are stricter. Directors must undergo background checks and disclose any prior legal proceedings. This increases accountability in board composition.

Non-compliance with these requirements can result in penalties, director disqualification, and reputational damage. Institutional investors and stock exchanges are scrutinizing governance disclosures more closely. Compliance is both a legal requirement and a competitive concern.

Companies should review board composition and related party transaction processes. Audit disclosure practices and ensure systems capture the newly required information. Governance counsel can assist in implementing compliant structures.
