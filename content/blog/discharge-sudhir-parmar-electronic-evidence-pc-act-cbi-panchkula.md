By order dated 20.04.2026 in **State v. Sudhir Parmar and Others**, PC/03/2026 (FIR No. 0006 dated 17.04.2023, P.S. ACB, Panchkula), the Court of Sh. Rajeev Goyal, Special Judge (CBI), Haryana at Panchkula has discharged all five accused, including the former Special Judge of the CBI/PMLA Court, Haryana at Panchkula, in a case alleging the demand and acceptance of bribes of Rs.5 to 7 Crores. The Court held that absent disclosure of the original source of the electronic evidence, production of the recording device, and a certificate under Section 65-B of the Indian Evidence Act, the whatsapp chats and audio recordings supplied by an anonymous source could not be looked into even at the charge framing stage.

## The FIR and the Charges

The case arose from a source information report received by the State Vigilance and Anti-Corruption Bureau, Haryana containing whatsapp screenshots and audio recordings allegedly involving the then Special Judge, CBI/PMLA. After permission of the Hon'ble Chief Justice of the High Court of Punjab and Haryana, FIR No. 06 dated 17.04.2023 was registered under Sections 7, 8, 11 and 13 of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 read with Section 120-B of the Indian Penal Code. The final report named five accused: the former Judicial Officer, his nephew, the promoter of M3M Group, the Managing Director of IREO Group, and the owner of Vatika Limited.

## The Charge-Framing Standard

Tracing the law through **Union of India v. Prafulla Kumar Samal, (1979) 3 SCC 4**, **P. Vijayan v. State of Kerala, (2010) 2 SCC 398** and **State of Gujarat v. Dilipsinh Kishorsinh Rao, (2023) 17 SCC 688**, the Court reiterated that the Judge may sift and weigh evidence to find a prima facie case but cannot act as a post office for the prosecution, that where two views are equally possible the accused is entitled to discharge, and that material from the charge sheet itself which drastically affects the sustainability of the prosecution case must be considered at this stage.

## Why the Electronic Evidence Was Held Unreliable

The Court found two infirmities to be fatal. First, although the seized mobile phones were sent for forensic examination and the entire data including deleted material was retrieved, the alleged incriminating whatsapp chats were not found in the retrieved data. The investigating agency's justification, that the chats may have been deleted or overlapped over time, was rejected as baseless because even deleted data had been recovered.

Second, all twelve audio recordings were not originals but copies stored on a single date, 26.01.2023, in an Olympus Digital Voice Recorder and a CD-R. The original device on which the recordings were made was never produced or sent for forensic examination. The CFSL report opined the voices to be merely "probable" matches and matched only four of the twelve files. The witness present during the conversations denied having made the recordings. The maker of the recordings was, in the Court's words, unidentified, and the integrity of the source could not be ruled out.

The Court placed reliance on **Nilesh Dinkar Paradkar v. State of Maharashtra, (2011) 4 SCC 143**, **Ziyauddin Burhanuddin Bukhari v. Brijmohan Ramdass Mehra, (1976) 2 SCC 17** and **Ram Singh v. Col. Ram Singh, 1985 Supp SCC 611** for the settled requirement that audio recordings are admissible only on proof of identification of the voice, accuracy of what was recorded, exclusion of every possibility of tampering, and safe custody. The prosecution was also unable to file a certificate under Section 65-B of the Indian Evidence Act since it was not even aware of the original device or digital record source. Drawing on **Karan Talwar v. State of Tamil Nadu, 2024 SCC OnLine SC 3803**, the Court held that where electronic evidence cannot be translated into admissible evidence at trial, no prima-facie case can be said to be made out for charge.

## The Collateral Allegations

Allegations of benami acquisition of property, undervalued sales, friendly loans transferred without documentation to the family members of the former Judicial Officer, recovery of cash during search, and a hike in the salary of his nephew were each found to rest on the bedrock of the electronic evidence and to fall with it. Independent of that evidence, undervaluation could at best attract action under the Indian Stamp Act, 1899, and the offence under Section 13(1)(b) of the Prevention of Corruption Act, 1988 was not attracted because no Disproportionate Assets investigation, with a defined check period and an income and expenditure analysis, had been conducted.

## The Discharge

Holding that the electronic evidence could not be translated into admissible evidence at trial and that the material on record did not give rise even to grave suspicion, the Court discharged all five accused.
