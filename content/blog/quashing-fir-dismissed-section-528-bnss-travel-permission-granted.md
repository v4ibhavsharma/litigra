The Punjab and Haryana High Court in **Virender Kumar v. State of Punjab** (CRM-M-34931-2024) examined the appropriate exercise of discretion where a petition seeking quashing of an FIR under Section 528 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita was dismissed, yet the court granted conditional relief permitting the petitioner limited travel permissions. The decision illustrates the spectrum of remedies available to courts and the flexibility with which discretionary relief may be fashioned even where the primary relief sought is declined.

## The Petition for Quashing and Its Dismissal

**Mr. Virender Kumar** filed a petition under Section 528 of the Bharatiya Nyaya Sanhita (the criminal procedure statute that supersedes the Criminal Procedure Code of 1973) seeking the quashing of an FIR registered against him. Section 528 of BNSS is the successor provision to former Section 482 of the CrPC and permits high courts to exercise inherent powers to quash criminal proceedings where the charge discloses no offence or where it is a case where the investigation or prosecution is manifestly frivolous or vexatious.

The High Court, upon examination of the FIR and the allegations contained therein, found that the petition for quashing did not meet the stringent requirements under Section 528 BNSS. The allegations, if accepted, did disclose an offence, and the court could not conclude that the prosecution was manifestly frivolous or vexatious. The petition was accordingly dismissed, and the case would proceed to investigation and trial.

## The Nature of Discretionary Relief

However, the dismissal of the quashing petition did not mark the end of the court's engagement with the petitioner's circumstances. The High Court recognized that even where a petition for quashing is rejected, the court retains discretion to grant ancillary or conditional relief that addresses specific concerns or hardships of the petitioner without undermining the investigative or prosecution process. This recognition reflects an understanding that judicial discretion is not exhausted by the binary acceptance or rejection of the main petition.

## The Grant of Conditional Travel Permission

Recognizing the petitioner's need for mobility while ensuring his continued availability for investigation and trial, the High Court exercised its discretion to grant **Mr. Virender Kumar** permission to travel, subject to stringent conditions. The conditions imposed were calculated to protect the interests of the investigating agency and the state while permitting the petitioner limited freedom of movement.

The conditional travel permission typically requires the petitioner to provide prior intimation to the investigating officer before travelling, to submit to identification and verification procedures, and to maintain regular contact with investigating authorities. Travel permissions may be confined to specified destinations or permitted upon submission of personal recognizance or bail bonds. Such conditions ensure that the petitioner remains within the framework of the criminal process even while exercising limited freedom of movement.

## Balancing Competing Interests

The grant of conditional travel permission reflects a nuanced judicial approach that balances the petitioner's legitimate interest in mobility against the state's interest in ensuring the success of investigation and prosecution. The criminal law recognizes that unrestricted prevention of movement pending investigation or trial can cause substantial hardship, particularly to individuals whose livelihood or personal circumstances require travel. Simultaneously, the state has a legitimate interest in ensuring that accused persons do not abscond or evade the criminal process.

Conditional travel permission represents a middle ground that accommodates both interests. The petitioner obtains limited relief even though the principal petition failed, while the state retains adequate safeguards to ensure the continuity of the criminal process.

## Principled Exercise of Discretion

This decision underscores that judicial discretion in the criminal sphere is not confined to rigid categories of acceptance or rejection. Courts possess inherent authority to fashion remedies tailored to the circumstances of each case, provided that such remedies do not fundamentally undermine the investigative or prosecutorial process. The grant of conditional relief, short of the primary remedy sought, demonstrates the court's commitment to principled but flexible jurisprudence in criminal matters.
