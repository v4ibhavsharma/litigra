The Civil Court at Chandigarh in **Amarjit Singh Chatha v. Punjab & Sind Bank** (CS-327-2019) addressed the dismissal of a plaint on two distinct yet complementary grounds: lack of territorial jurisdiction and the statutory bar against injunctions under Section 41(h) of the Specific Relief Act. The judgment demonstrates the careful interplay between procedural prerequisites and substantive law principles that govern civil litigation.

## The Facts and Cause of Action

**Mr. Amarjit Singh Chatha** filed a suit seeking an injunction against **Punjab & Sind Bank** to restrain the bank from conducting an auction of a property. The underlying dispute arose from a mortgage transaction where the bank, as mortgagee, initiated auction proceedings against the mortgaged property. The plaintiff sought preventative relief to stay the auction. However, a critical issue emerged: the property subject to the proposed auction was situated in Punjab, whereas the suit was filed at Chandigarh.

## The Jurisdictional Impediment

Under the Civil Procedure Code, territorial jurisdiction is a fundamental prerequisite for the entertainment of suits. The court in which a suit is filed must have jurisdiction over the subject-matter and the territory where the cause of action arose or where the property in dispute is located. Section 17 of the CPC establishes that suits concerning immovable property must ordinarily be filed in the court within whose jurisdiction the property is situated.

In the present case, the court found that since the property was located in Punjab, the competent forum for filing the suit was in Punjab and not at Chandigarh. The filing of the suit at Chandigarh constituted a clear violation of the territorial jurisdiction principles. This defect could not be cured by the court's mere inclination to address the merits of the dispute.

## The Statutory Bar Under Section 41(h) SRA

Beyond the jurisdictional infirmity, the court identified a substantive impediment to the grant of injunction under the Specific Relief Act. Section 41(h) of the Specific Relief Act precludes the grant of an injunction where the plaintiff has an equally efficacious alternative remedy available at law. The court reasoned that in the context of auction proceedings, statutory remedies such as redemption before the auction sale is finalized or remedies available under the mortgage laws and the relevant recovery laws provide adequate protection to the mortgagor.

The bank's auction process itself contained built-in safeguards and procedural steps that afforded the mortgagor meaningful opportunity to challenge or settle the proceedings before finalization. Consequently, the court held that the plaintiff could not be granted injunctive relief where such alternative remedies existed and were equally capable of providing the requisite protection.

## The Plaint's Rejection

By Order 7 Rule 11 of the Civil Procedure Code, a plaint must contain all necessary particulars and the plaintiff must have a cause of action capable of being entertained by the court. The convergence of lack of territorial jurisdiction and the statutory bar under Section 41(h) rendered the plaint liable to rejection at the threshold stage itself, without requiring the court to proceed to trial or final adjudication.

## Principles for Litigants

This decision serves as a cautionary reminder to litigants and their counsel that suits must be instituted in the competent court and that relief sought must not fall within the statutory exclusions enumerated in the Specific Relief Act. Injunctive relief, though powerful, is not available as a matter of right and must yield to statutory limitations and the availability of alternative remedies.
