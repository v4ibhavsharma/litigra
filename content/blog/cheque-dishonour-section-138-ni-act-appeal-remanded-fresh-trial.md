The Punjab and Haryana High Court in **Abhishek Gautam v. Tirupathi Stainless Steel** (CRA-AS-5977-2018) examined the critical issue of dishonest intent in cheque dishonour prosecutions under Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act. The Court's decision underscores the necessity of meticulous examination of evidence regarding the mental element required to establish culpability in such cases.

## Background of the Case

**Mr. Abhishek Gautam** filed a complaint against **Tirupathi Stainless Steel** alleging dishonour of a cheque issued by the respondent. The case arose from a commercial transaction where the cheque was presented but dishonoured due to insufficient funds. The trial court, after examining the evidence, rendered an order of acquittal, finding insufficient proof of the necessary elements constituting the offence under Section 138 NI Act.

## Legal Framework Under Section 138

Section 138 of the Negotiable Instruments Act prescribes punishment for cheque dishonour and requires that the drawer possessed knowledge that the cheque would be dishonoured. The provision contemplates a three-tier structure: presentation of the cheque within six months, its dishonour, and notice of the same to the drawer. Critically, the prosecution must establish that the drawer issued the cheque with intent to defraud or with knowledge that the account lacked sufficient funds.

## Trial Court's Analysis and Error

The trial court acquitted the accused, apparently finding that the evidence did not conclusively demonstrate dishonest intent. However, upon appellate scrutiny, the High Court found that the trial court had not conducted a sufficiently rigorous appraisal of the available evidence. The court noted that critical documents and circumstances bearing upon the mental state of the drawer had been inadequately considered or weighed.

## High Court's Intervention and Remand

The High Court intervened on the ground that the trial court had failed to properly appreciate the evidentiary foundation for establishing dishonest intent. Rather than upholding the acquittal or reversing it outright, the Court remanded the matter for fresh consideration by the trial court. This approach permitted the trial court to undertake a more thorough and careful examination of the evidence with specific attention to the mental element of the offence.

## Significance for NI Act Prosecutions

This decision reinforces that prosecutions under Section 138 demand careful judicial scrutiny of evidence addressing the subjective ingredient of dishonest intent. Trial courts are expected to actively engage with evidence of the drawer's knowledge and intention rather than mechanically applying the statutory framework. The remand illustrates the appellate court's supervisory role in ensuring that the legal burden of proof is properly discharged.
